3 Simple Mass-specific Rates

This chapter concerns one and two compartment models with simple mass-specific fluxes.

3.1 Learning Objectives

  • use simple 1- and 2-compartment models to track and predict fates of elements.
  • use first order equations to represent inflows and outflows (fluxes) that are constant.
  • calculate rates of change of mass in compartments, time to events, and make predictions about future states.

Key concepts

  • First-order kinetics: The rate of a process is proportional to the amount present.
  • General form: \[\frac{dX}{dt} = \mathrm{Inputs} - k X\]

where \(k\) is the rate constant.

The units of \(k\) are “amount of \(X\) transferred per unit time per amount of \(X\) in the pool”, or

\[\mathrm{units}(k) = \frac{\mathrm{units}(X)}{\mathrm{units}(X)} \cdot \frac{1}{\mathrm{units}(t)}\] or, which simplifies to, \[\mathrm{units}(k) = \mathrm{units}(t)\]

For instance, imagine the rate water draining out of a bathtub per second is a function of the amount of the water in liters. The units of \(k\) would be “liters per liter per second” \[ \begin{align} \mathrm{units}(k) &= \frac{\mathrm{L}}{\mathrm{L} \cdot s}\\ &= \frac{1}{s} \end{align} \]

simplifying to just “per second”.

The fraction lost per unit time is constant, but the absolute amount lost decreases as the pool size declines.

More key concepts

Read the following now, but apply them later as you work through the examples. Don’t sweat these too much to start. They will make more sense as you work through the examples.

  1. STEADY STATE: At steady state, inputs = outputs
    • For first-order loss: Input = k*N_ss
    • Therefore: N_ss = Input/k
  2. MULTIPLE FIRST-ORDER LOSSES: Rate constants are additive
    • k_total = k₁ + k₂ + k₃ + …
    • N_ss = Input/k_total
  3. RESIDENCE TIME: Average time a particle spends in the pool
    • τ = N_ss/Input = 1/k_total
    • Larger k means faster turnover, shorter residence time
  4. APPROACH TO STEADY STATE:
    • If N > N_ss: dN/dt < 0 (pool declining toward steady state)
    • If N < N_ss: dN/dt > 0 (pool growing toward steady state)
  5. PARTITIONING LOSSES: At steady state, each pathway processes a fraction of the input proportional to its rate constant:
    • Fraction through pathway i = k_i/k_total

Figure 3.1: A one compartment model.

For a one-compartment model of element \(X\) and first order kinetics, we still have one or more fluxes and out. What is new is that those fluxes are functions of the size of the pool. So we still have the rate of change in the mass of \(X\) is \[\frac{dX}{dt} = F_{in} - F_{out}\], and now also where, \[F_{out} = kX\]

where flux units must match, for instance, units(\(F_{in}\)) \(= units(\)F_{in}\() \mathrm{kg\cdot y^{-1}}\), or kilograms per year, so units(\(k\)) = year.

3.2 Solving rate equations

We often want to figure out whether a system is at an equilibrium or might achieve an equilibrium in the future. We call this equilibrium a steady state because the amounts in the pools remain “steady”, or at constant amounts. That is, the state of the system remains constant, steady, unchanging. In other words, the rate of change, \(dX/dt\), is zero.

In general, we “solve” a rate equation by first setting it equal to zero, and then solving for \(X\) (the state variable):

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{dX}{dt} &= \mathrm{Inputs} - k X\\ 0 &= \mathrm{Inputs} - k X\\ k X &= \mathrm{Inputs}\\ X &= \frac{\mathrm{Inputs}}{k} \end{align*} \] What is this telling us?

It is telling us that the amount of \(X\) will stop changing when \(X\) reaches the amount predicted by the fraction \(\mathrm{Inputs}/k\).

You should work through the units on this to convince yourself the the units in each term are the same.

To solve a rate equation with constant rates, is either simpler or less intuitive, depending on how you look at it. Essentially, when all the fluxes are constant, the only way we get a steady state is when the inflows are precisely equal to the outflows. Otherwise, a system with constant rates is predicted to forever increase or forever decrease, depending on the balance of inflows and outflows. If you walk through the arithematic we did above, you would find that the only way that the rate equation can equal zero is if the pool is empty (\(X=0\)) or \(F_{in}=F_{out}\).

3.3 One-compartment models

3.3.1 Worked Example: Lake phosphorus with flushing

A lake receives constant phosphorus input from a wastewater treatment plant but loses phosphorus proportional to the amount present due to water outflow.

  • Constant input: 100 kg P/year
  • First-order loss rate constant: k = 0.4 per year

The lake currently contains 300 kg P.

Questions: 1. Draw a picture of the system. 1. Write the differential equation for dP/dt 1. Calculate dP/dt at the current phosphorus level 1. Is the lake at steady state? 1. What phosphorus level would represent steady state? (‘Solve’ the differential equation) 1. Is the lake moving toward or away from steady state?

Figure 3.2: Picgture of a one compartment model of phosphorus in a lake.

Differential equation \[\frac{dP}{dt} = I - kP\] where \(I = 100 \mathrm{kg~P}/y\), \(k=0.4 y^{-1}\), and the initial state is \(P_0 = 300 \mathrm{kg~P}\).

dP/dt at the current phosphorus level \[\frac{dP}{dt} = I - kP_0 = 100-0.4(300)= -20\, \mathrm{kg~P}\cdot y^{-1}\]

Is the lake at steady state?

The lake \(P\) is not at steady state because the current rate of change (at \(P=300\)) is nonzero (negative,$ -20, y^{-1}$.

To determine the amount of phosphorus that would be the steady state, or tequilibrium, we solve \(dP/dt = 0\)for \(P\).

What phosphorus level would represent steady state?

To answer this question, we solve the ddifferential equation. That is, we set the equation equal to zero, and then solve for the state variable, in this case \(P\). Why do we set it to zero? We set it equal to zero because a value of zero means that the rate of change of \(P\) is zero - the amount of phosphorus in the lake is not changing and so exists at a steady state.

By solving for \(P\), we are discovering the value of \(P\) when the system is at a steady state.

To determine the amount of phosphorus that would be the steady state, or the equilibrium, we solve \(dP/dt = 0\) for \(P\). Notice that the initial state doesn’t matter.

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{dP}{dt} &= I - kP\\ 0&= I - kP\\ kP &= I \\ P^* &= \frac{I}{k} = \frac{100}{0.4} = 250\,\mathrm{kg~P} \end{align*} \] The star or asterisk on \(P^*\) signals that this is an equilibrium or steady state.

Is the lake moving toward or away from steady state?

Because the lake \(P\) is declining from 300 kg, it is moving toward it’s steady state.

A forested watershed receives nitrogen through atmospheric deposition at a constant rate but loses nitrogen through leaching and stream export:

  • Constant atmospheric deposition: 7 kg N/ha/year
  • Leaching loss rate constant: k = 0.16 per year

The soil solution currently contains 26 kg N/ha.

Questions:

  1. Draw a picture of the system.
  2. Write the differential equation for dN/dt
  3. Calculate dN/dt at the current nitrogen level
  4. Is the soil solution at steady state?
  5. What nitrogen level would represent steady state? (‘Solve’ the differential equation)
  6. Is the soil solution moving toward or away from steady state?

3.4.2 Example 2: Soil Organic Matter Decomposition

A soil layer receives organic matter from plant litter at a constant rate but decomposes following first-order kinetics.

  • Constant litterfall input: 500 g C/m²/year
  • Decomposition rate constant: k = 0.25 per year

The soil currently contains 1,800 g C/m².

Questions:

  1. Write the differential equation for dC/dt
  2. Calculate dC/dt at the current carbon level
  3. What is the steady-state carbon pool size?
  4. How much carbon is being lost through decomposition right now?
  5. At steady state, how much carbon would be lost per year through decomposition?

3.4.3 Example 3: Forest Canopy Nitrogen with Multiple Losses

A forest canopy receives nitrogen through atmospheric deposition at a constant rate but loses nitrogen through multiple first-order processes:

  • Constant atmospheric deposition: 8 kg N/ha/year
  • Leaching loss rate constant: k₁ = 0.15 per year
  • Herbivory loss rate constant: k₂ = 0.08 per year
  • Litterfall rate constant: k₃ = 0.35 per year

The canopy currently contains 120 kg N/ha.

Questions:

  1. Write the differential equation for dN/dt
  2. What is the total loss rate constant (k_total)?
  3. Calculate dN/dt at the current nitrogen level
  4. Calculate the steady-state nitrogen pool size
  5. At steady state, how much nitrogen is lost through each pathway annually?
  6. What percentage of the canopy nitrogen pool turns over each year?

3.4.4 Example 4: Stream Dissolved Organic Carbon

A stream reach receives dissolved organic carbon (DOC) from upstream at a constant rate, but processes it through multiple pathways:

  • Upstream DOC input: 2,400 g C/day
  • Microbial respiration rate constant: k₁ = 0.5 per day
  • Photodegradation rate constant: k₂ = 0.2 per day
  • Downstream export: Flow rate (Q) = 10,000 L/day, Stream volume (V) = 50,000 L (Export rate constant k₃ = Q/V)

The stream currently contains 6,000 g C.

Questions:

  1. Calculate the export rate constant k₃
  2. Write the differential equation for dC/dt with all loss terms
  3. What is the total loss rate constant?
  4. Calculate dC/dt at the current DOC level
  5. What is the steady-state DOC pool?
  6. At steady state, how much carbon exits through each pathway daily?
  7. What is the residence time of DOC in this stream reach at steady state? (Residence time τ = N_ss / F_in)

3.5 Two-compartment models

When we couple two compartments with first-order transfer, the rate of transfer between compartments is proportional to the amount in the source compartment.

General form for two compartments:

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{dN_1}{dt} &= F_{in} - k_{21}N_1 - k_{1,out}N_1\\ \frac{dN_2}{dt} &= k_{21}N_1 - k_{2,out}N_2 \end{align*} \]

where:

  • \(k_{21}\) = rate constant for transfer into compartment 2 from compartment 1
  • \(k_{1,out}\) = rate constant for loss from compartment 1 to outside
  • \(k_{2,out}\) = rate constant for loss from compartment 2 to outside

Note that both rates have a shared term, \(k_{21}N_1\). This is an export from from one compartment and an import into the other, and its rate is proportional to the source.

At steady state: both \(dN_1/dt = 0\) AND \(dN_2/dt = 0\).

3.5.1 Key concepts

Having connected or coupled compartments allows us to take advantage of several additional concepts.

  1. COUPLED EQUATIONS: With first-order exchange, compartments are mathematically linked. Changes in one affect the other.
  2. SOLVING FOR STEADY STATE:
    • Set both \(dN_1/dt = 0\) and \(dN_2/dt = 0\).
    • Solve the system of simultaneous equations
    • Usually solve one equation for a ratio, then substitute
  3. BIDIRECTIONAL EXCHANGE: When compartments exchange material both ways, both differential equations include terms from the other compartment
  4. MASS BALANCE CHECK: At steady state:
    • Total system input = total system output
    • Fluxes between compartments must balance
  5. RESIDENCE TIME: More complex with exchange
    • Need to account for all inputs to a compartment
    • Including both external and internal (exchange) inputs
  6. NET FLUX: With bidirectional exchange, calculate both directions then find net = larger flux - smaller flux
  7. APPROACH TO STEADY STATE: Both compartments move toward their steady-state values, but at potentially different rates.

3.6 Answer Key

Example 1: Watershed Nitrogen Loss

A forested watershed receives nitrogen through atmospheric deposition at a constant rate but loses nitrogen through leaching and stream export:

  • Constant atmospheric deposition: 7 kg N/ha/year
  • Leaching loss rate constant: k = 0.16 per year

The soil solution currently contains 26 kg N/ha.

Questions:

  1. A picture of the system.
  2. \(dN/dt = I_D - kN\)
  3. \(dN/dt = 7 - 0.16(26) = 2.84\)
  4. The soil solution at steady state is not at steady state, but is increasing.
  5. What nitrogen level would represent steady state?

\[ \begin{align*} \frac{dN/dt} = 0 &= I_D - kN\\ kN &=I_D\\ N &= \frac{I_D}{k} = 7/0.16 = 43.75 \end{align*} \] in units of kg N/ha.

  1. The soil solution is moving toward steady state.

Example 2: Soil Organic Matter

  1. dC/dt = 500 - 0.25C

  2. dC/dt = 500 - 0.25(1,800) = 500 - 450 = 50 g C/m²/year

    Soil carbon is accumulating

  3. At steady state:

0 = 500 - 0.25C_ss C_ss = 500/0.25 = 2,000 g C/m²

  1. Current decomposition rate = k*C = 0.25(1,800) = 450 g C/m²/year

  2. At steady state, decomposition must equal input = 500 g C/m²/year (Check: 0.25 × 2,000 = 500)

Example 3: Forest Canopy Nitrogen

  1. dN/dt = 8 - k₁N - k₂N - k₃N = 8 - (k₁ + k₂ + k₃)N dN/dt = 8 - (0.15 + 0.08 + 0.35)N = 8 - 0.58N

  2. k_total = 0.15 + 0.08 + 0.35 = 0.58 per year

  3. dN/dt = 8 - 0.58(120) = 8 - 69.6 = -61.6 kg N/ha/year The canopy is losing nitrogen

  4. At steady state: 0 = 8 - 0.58*N_ss N_ss = 8/0.58 = 13.8 kg N/ha

  5. At steady state (N = 13.8 kg N/ha):

    • Leaching: 0.15 × 13.8 = 2.07 kg N/ha/year
    • Herbivory: 0.08 × 13.8 = 1.10 kg N/ha/year
    • Litterfall: 0.35 × 13.8 = 4.83 kg N/ha/year
    • Total: 2.07 + 1.10 + 4.83 = 8.0 kg N/ha/year ✓

  6. Turnover rate = k_total = 0.58 per year = 58% per year

Example 4: Stream DOC

  1. k₃ = Q/V = 10,000/50,000 = 0.2 per day

  2. dC/dt = 2,400 - k₁C - k₂C - k₃C dC/dt = 2,400 - (0.5 + 0.2 + 0.2)C dC/dt = 2,400 - 0.9*C

  3. k_total = 0.5 + 0.2 + 0.2 = 0.9 per day

  4. dC/dt = 2,400 - 0.9(6,000) = 2,400 - 5,400 = -3,000 g C/day Stream is losing DOC

  5. At steady state: 0 = 2,400 - 0.9*C_ss C_ss = 2,400/0.9 = 2,667 g C

  6. At steady state (C = 2,667 g C):

    • Respiration: 0.5 × 2,667 = 1,333 g C/day
    • Photodegradation: 0.2 × 2,667 = 533 g C/day
    • Export: 0.2 × 2,667 = 533 g C/day
    • Total: 1,333 + 533 + 533 = 2,400 g C/day ✓

  7. Residence time τ = N_ss / F_in = 2,667 / 2,400 = 1.11 days

    Alternative calculation: τ = 1/k_total = 1/0.9 = 1.11 days

    On average, a carbon atom spends 1.11 days in the stream reach.
